1. Was Fukushima preventable?
Follow-up: What single decision would have made the biggest difference?

2. Have you ever stayed silent about a problem because it wasn’t your responsibility?
Follow-up: When responsibility is spread across governments, regulators, and corporations, does the system allow avoidance of accountability? Who designs and benefits from such a system?

3. Society accepts car accident deaths as a trade-off for driving’s benefits. Is nuclear power fundamentally different, or are its risks incomparable?

4. How should societies decide when disaster prevention becomes an unreasonable cost?
Follow-up: Before Fukushima, skipping seawall upgrades seemed prudent. After, it seems negligent. Should risk decisions be judged by outcomes or what was knowable at the time?

5. After the disaster, TEPCO was nationalized to prevent collapse. Is protecting a culpable corporation ever justifiable, or does it break the link between actions and consequences?

6. Fukushima’s cleanup will take decades, paid for by those with no say in the decisions. Do engineers have a moral obligation to future generations?
Follow-up: When does shifting risks onto the future stop being an engineering decision and become unethical?

7. Plant manager Masao Yoshida defied TEPCO orders, likely averting worse disaster. Can we expect engineers to prioritize public safety over loyalty when institutions make defiance so costly?
Follow-up: If systems punish whistleblowers and protect silence, can individual courage ever prevent systemic failure?

8. What does Fukushima reveal about the meaning of being an ethical engineer?
Is ethics just following rules, or does it require something deeper—beyond what codes and institutions enforce?
