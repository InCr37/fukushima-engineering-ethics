2. Think of a time you stayed silent about a problem because it wasn't "officially" your responsibility. What made that feel acceptable in the moment? follow up. When responsibility is distributed across governments, regulators, and corporations — each pointing to the others — does the system itself become a mechanism for escaping accountability? And if so, who designed that system, and who benefits from it?
3. Society accepts that car accidents will kill thousands every year because we've decided the benefit of driving outweighs the cost. Is nuclear power any different — or is there something about the nature of nuclear risk that makes it categorically incomparable?
4. Every dollar spent preventing a disaster that may never happen is a dollar not spent on healthcare, education, or infrastructure that will affect people. How should societies decide when precaution becomes an unreasonable burden? follow up: Before Fukushima, skipping the seawall upgrades looked like a sensible financial decision. After, it looks criminal. Is it fair to judge risk management decisions by their outcomes rather than by what was reasonably knowable at the time?
5. TEPCO was effectively nationalized after the disaster to prevent it from collapsing — meaning the company responsible for the disaster was protected by the state. Is shielding a culpable corporation from bankruptcy ever justifiable, or does it fundamentally corrupt the relationship between consequence and responsibility?
6. ⁠The Fukushima cleanup will not be completed until 2051 at the earliest — paid for by people who had no voice in the decisions that made it necessary. Do engineers and institutions have a moral obligation to future generations who cannot yet advocate for themselves — and if so, how should that obligation be weighed against present-day pressures and costs?
Follow up: At what point does passing risk onto future generations stop being an engineering decision and become an ethical violation?
7. Plant manager Masao Yoshida defied direct orders from TEPCO headquarters and continued seawater injection into the reactor — a decision that likely prevented a far worse catastrophe. Engineering codes of conduct demand public safety above organizational loyalty. But in practice, careers, livelihoods, and institutional cultures make defiance enormously costly. Is it realistic — or even fair — to expect individual engineers to act against their organization when institutions themselves are the problem?
Follow up: If the system punishes those who speak up and protects those who stay silent, can individual courage ever be a reliable safeguard against institutional failure?
8. Across everything we've discussed — systemic accountability, acceptable risk, corporate consequence, generational responsibility, and the cost of silence — what does Fukushima tell us about what it truly means to be an ethical engineer?
Is ethics simply following a code of conduct — or does it demand something harder? Something that codes, regulations, and institutions cannot enforce?
