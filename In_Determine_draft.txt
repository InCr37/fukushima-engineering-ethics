1. Faster, Better, Cheaper vs Safety #-> authority

TEPCO operated under a business logic: new safety regulations would interfere with operations and reduce profits. When experts proposed tsunami countermeasures, TEPCO argued these would be "too expensive" and "impractical." The Federation of Electric Power Companies lobby-against stronger regulations. This reflects WFEO's principle that ** engineers shall reject practices that harm public welfare**. Safety cannot be a variable in the cost-quality-speed equation — but at Fukushima, it was.

2. "**Engineers** must not only ensure systems don't fail — they must also ensure that when failures occur, damage is limited and recovery is fast."

Engineers have to assume crysis hit in any second and have at least some preparation for it.

The official did not make a plan to handle what exactly to do with smooth and order, once the nuclear plant is blew up.

TEPCO's approach: "We've made the plant safe — we don't need to plan for failure."

This is arrogant engineering — assuming your preventions are perfect.

e.g. house prevent fire but also do fire drill

Resilient engineering asks:

What happens when multiple systems fail simultaneously?
How do we limit damage when failures occur?
How do we recover quickly when damage occurs?
How do we communicate and coordinate when infrastructure fails?
