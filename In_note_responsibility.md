# Responsibility Analysis: Who Did What Wrong

## Mistakes by Party

### 1. TEPCO (Tokyo Electric Power Company)

| Mistake | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **Ignored tsunami warnings since 2006** | No countermeasures taken despite knowing risk |
| **Delayed/cancelled anti-seismic reinforcements** | Deadline pushed from 2009 to 2016; zero reinforcements done |
| **Resisted new safety regulations** | Used Federation of Electric Power Companies to lobby against regulations |
| **Put profit over safety** | "New regulations would interfere with operations" |
| **Manual IC shutdown in Unit 1** | Left reactor without cooling much earlier than necessary |
| **Poor emergency training** | Only 10-15% of nearby residents knew about accident possibility |
| **Failed to disclose SPEEDI data** | People evacuated into high radiation areas |
| **Inadequate worker protection** | ~30% of workers never told their radiation dose; forced to share dosimeters |
| **Management style** | "Government should take final responsibility" — avoided accountability |

---

### 2. NISA (Nuclear and Industrial Safety Agency) — Regulator

| Mistake | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **Accepting TEPCO's delayed deadline** | No enforcement of 2009 deadline |
| **Tacit consent for delay** | Allowed significant delay without consequences |
| **No independent safety standards** | Relied on operators to "self-regulate" |
| **Part of METI, not independent** | Couldn't act against nuclear industry interests |
| **Ignored international safety knowledge** | "Negative attitude toward importation of new advances" |

---

### 3. METI (Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry) — Nuclear Promoter

| Mistake | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **Made NISA part of their ministry** | Created structural conflict of interest |
| **Prioritized nuclear energy policy over safety** | Regulators not independent |
| **Political pressure on regulators** | Prevented effective oversight |

---

### 4. Nuclear Safety Commission (NSC)

| Mistake | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **Approved outdated safety guidelines** | Chairman Madarame admitted guidelines were defective |
| **No enforcement authority** | Weak oversight structure |
| **Didn't require severe accident measures** | Japan had SA countermeasures "practically ineffective" |

---

### 5. Government / Kantei (Prime Minister's Office)

| Mistake | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **Disrupted chain of command** | Contacted plant directly instead of through regional emergency team |
| **Slow to declare emergency** | Delayed official response |
| **Failed to communicate severity** | "Only 20% knew about accident when evacuation ordered" |
| **Didn't disclose SPEEDI data** | Residents couldn't make informed decisions |
| **No clear role boundaries** | Government, regulators, TEPCO all unclear who was in charge |
| **No contingency for multi-unit disaster** | "First multi-unit accident in power reactor history" |

---

### 6. Masao Yoshida (Plant Manager)

| Decision | Impact |
|----------|--------|
| **Disobeyed TEPCO HQ to continue seawater injection** | Probably prevented worse disaster |
| **Continued cooling despite orders to stop** | Critical judgment call that saved lives |

*Note: Yoshida's decisions were largely correct. His "mistakes" were minor compared to others' failures.*

---

### 7. Plant Workers (General)

| Issue | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| **No consent for accident response** | ~40% of subcontracted workers didn't agree to participate |
| **No dosimeters** | Couldn't track radiation exposure |
| **No information** | Didn't know how dangerous conditions were until March 15 |
| **Inadequate briefings** | ~20% received no radiation level information |

---

### 8. System-Wide / Cultural Failures

| Failure | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Regulatory capture** | "Nuclear power village" — revolving door between industry and regulators |
| **Normalization of deviance** | Unsafe practices became acceptable over time |
| **Groupism / refusal to question authority** | Chairman Kurokawa: "reflexive obedience" |
| **Production pressure** | Safety sacrificed for operational efficiency |
| **No independent oversight** | All parties colluded to avoid scrutiny |
| **Failure to learn from warnings** | Multiple experts warned about tsunami risk, ignored |

| Party | What They Knew | What They Did |
|-------|---------------|---------------|
| **TEPCO** | Tsunami risk since 2006 | Delayed countermeasures |
| **Regulators** | Risk of total SBO | Accepted delay, didn't enforce |
| **Government** | Earthquake/tsunami predictable | Prioritized nuclear policy |
| **Experts** | Warned since 2003 | Ignored, lawsuits failed |

## The Core Failure

The NAIIC report sums it up:

> *"This was a disaster 'Made in Japan.' Its fundamental causes are to be found in the ingrained conventions of Japanese culture: our reflexive obedience; our reluctance to question authority; our devotion to 'sticking with the program'; our groupism; and our insularity."*

**But this isn't just Japan.** The same patterns appear in disasters worldwide:

| Disaster | Key Failure |
|----------|-------------|
| **Bhopal** | Regulations ignored, warnings dismissed |
| **Challenger** | Engineers' warnings overridden |
| **Texas City** | "This is not a safe plant" slide ignored |
| **Deepwater Horizon** | Halliburton warnings ignored |

---

## Universal Pattern

**The pattern is global: systems create incentives to ignore danger until catastrophe forces acknowledgment.**

| Stage | What Happens |
|-------|--------------|
| 1 | Warning signs appear |
| 2 | Business/organizational pressure to ignore |
| 3 | Regulatory capture or软弱 |
| 4 | Risk accumulates |
| 5 | Catastrophe occurs |
| 6 | Finger-pointing, but no systemic reform |
| 7 | Return to step 1 |

**The lesson**: Engineering disasters are almost never caused by a single bad actor. They are the predictable outcome of systems that reward risk-taking and punish those who raise concerns.
